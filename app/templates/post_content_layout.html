Application Mesh Service (AMS)

AMS is a platform that provides consistent visibility and network traffic controls of microservices that make up a cloud-native application. It is developed based on Istio service mesh and enables customers to secure, connect and monitor microservices, so they can modernize their enterprise apps more swiftly and securely.

When I joined the AMS product team in ByteDance, the team was working on delivering the MVP of AMS and on a stage that all features were built from zero to one. As a product management intern, I was responsible for the product design along with my mentor and delivered key features including service management, topological graph and payment, among which the topological graph was the most challenging one.

The topological graph visualizes the communication between different microservices in a graph. I started off proposing the feature by conducting competitor research and developing use cases and mainly focused on use cases of identifying services with errors and observing the status of a particular service. According to the target use cases, the topological graph enables users to find errors in an application quickly and proceed to take further actions like configuring routing rules to protect and improve the performance of their applications. I divided this feature development into two sprint iterations to make sure it provides basic functionalities in the MVP.

Although the project wasn't able to be delivered before I ended the internship due to dependency delays, my product designs were recognized by potential customers and attracted them to cooperate with us.

Payment Data Dashboard

Payment Data Dashboard is a tool that helps stakeholders track, analyze and display payment data in user-friendly ways.

When I worked in the payment team in ByteDance that provided payment ability to multiple business stakeholders, we didn't have a data dashboard to display payment metrics automatically. One of my responsibilities was to generate data from the database, make charts and share them with stakeholders. It usually took me more than 30 minutes to do so daily. So I decided to develop a data dashboard to improve our team's efficiency of data analysis.

At the beginning of the project, I collected metric requirements from all stakeholders and confirmed those metrics with our engineer. Then I proposed four dashboard sections to cover all needs including the overall pre-payment data, the overall automatic payment data, the payment details of each country and transaction errors. I scheduled a delivery plan with the data team and made sure the deliverable met the acceptance criteria.

After the dashboard was put into use, it saved us at least 30 minutes a day from the original workflow and empowered us to make data more transparent and accessible, measure payment performance and gain deeper insight.

Supporting Checkout with PayPal

This project enabled customers to use PayPal in our online Checkout.

When I worked in the payment team in ByteDance, we provided TikTok Ads with the Checkout section to enable customers to top-up their account balances. I started this project when we found the Checkout conversion rate was lower than expected in the US region, which means quite a lot of customers give up payment after they viewed the Checkout.

To improve the conversion, I studied the payment methods that our competitors provided with customers and found PayPal was one of their main payment methods in the US. I then communicated with the local sales team for their opinions of providing PayPal with our customers. After a discussion within our team, we decided to add PayPal to our Checkout in the US market as a trial to see customers' reactions and then consider adding it to other regions in later iterations.

After PayPal was supported, it turned out that around 30-40% of customers in the US prefer to use PayPal on our platform. The Checkout conversion rate increased from 60% to 65% on average within a month. It met our expectations. With this trial, we are more confident to support other local payment methods in other regions in future iterations.

Problem-Solving

There was a time when I found the requested feature didn't address the actual problem and was able to provide a better solution.

When I was responsible for collecting requests from stakeholders in the payment team, the sales team came up with a request to provide the automatic payment setting for non-self-serve customers. But I'm confused because the automatic payment setting is not designed for those non-self-serve customers who demand a large amount of ad campaigns on our platform.

I kept asking them why they would want to open the new payment setting and the actual problem turned out to be that they didn't feel secure to authenticate some customers of such a large amount of credit that the existing payment setting provided.

So instead of providing a new payment setting, we cut down the initial credit amount for the existing payment setting and make extra requirements for customers if they want to increase the credit amount.
